# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Junior developers and students encountering CI/CD and DevOps fundamentals for the first time. They arrive knowing how to write code but not yet how to ship it safely. Their context is learning: they may be following a course, trying to fix a messy deployment situation, or convincing their team to adopt pipelines. They're not reading reference docs — they're building a mental model.

## Product Purpose

Pipeline.dev is an educational landing page that teaches software delivery pipelines: what they are, why they matter, and how to build one. Success looks like a reader finishing the page with a clear, confident understanding of the pipeline flow — and the motivation to implement it.

## Brand Personality

Approachable, clear, encouraging.

The voice is a knowledgeable friend who happens to be a senior engineer: never condescending, never jargon-dense, never trying to impress. The goal is understanding, not authority performance.

## Anti-references

- **Startup SaaS landing pages**: no hero metric grids, no gradient CTAs, no "Trusted by 10,000 teams" rows. This is a teaching page, not a sales page.
- **Generic DevOps blogs**: no beige backgrounds, no stock photography, no listicle structure. The design should feel deliberate and worth reading.
- **Dense technical docs**: no text walls, no flat typographic hierarchy, no "just ship it and call it a day" layout. Every section should breathe.
- **Flashy dark mode**: the terminal aesthetic reinforces the subject matter — it's not a costume. No neon-on-black, no glow-everything, no cyber swagger. Restraint over spectacle.

## Design Principles

1. **Teach through clarity, not density.** The audience is building a mental model, not looking up reference. Every layout decision should reduce cognitive load: staged reveals, diagrams over paragraphs, breathing room over information packing.

2. **Earn credibility, don't perform it.** No social proof banners, no authority badges. The quality of the explanation is the credential. If the content is clear, the trust is implicit.

3. **Dark-mode with a purpose.** The terminal aesthetic earns its place because pipelines live in terminals, CI logs, and code editors. The darkness frames the content — it doesn't upstage it. Color is used to signal meaning (environment stages, severity, code) not decoration.

4. **Progressive disclosure.** Complex concepts unfold; they don't land all at once. Interactivity, diagrams, and section-by-section structure guide the reader through the mental model instead of front-loading everything.

5. **Approachability is non-negotiable.** The audience is early-career. The interface must never feel gatekeeping, intimidating, or "for experts only." Visually and tonally, the page says: "You can understand this."

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA. This means:
- 4.5:1 contrast ratio for normal text, 3:1 for large text
- Full keyboard navigation with visible focus states
- Screen reader support (semantic HTML, meaningful alt text, ARIA where needed)
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` for all animations
